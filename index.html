#2.4. Resolution Calculation

Our pinhole imaging point spread function is a convolution of geometrical and diffraction propagation. These two point spread function both related to the size of pinhole. The geometrical point spread function is given by the pinhole circle projection to the sensor plane. The optical intensity distribution from each pinhole onto the sensor plane is similar to Gaussian distribution. The geometrical point spread function can be calculated by Fraunhofer diffraction(first Bessel function). It is easy to find out that the optical intensity distribution caused by geometrical propagation is propotional to the size of pinhole which the diffraction propagation is inversely to.

Right now because of my limited basement in optics, the precise mathematical model of this point spread function is not established yet(individually calculate the geometrical resolution is higher than the actual data and diffraction one is lower than that). To calculate it, intensity relationship between these two propagation should be find out or a simulation based on Fourier optics should be established. In order to establish this simulation on Fourier optics, the transfer matrix of pinhole should be calculated first. Additionally, if computer hardware is upgraded in the future, consider using FDTD(Finite-Difference Time-Domain) to achieve it. To decide which area is the boundary of the distinguishable area, the Rayleigh criterion may help.

Some tips:

  • We use resolution board to test the actual resolution of our imaging system. Because of the existence of the difference between resolution board frequency, screen frequency, sensor sampling frequency and sensor pixel arrangement frequency, the Moire fringes may affect the accuracy of the experiment result.

  • A method to figure out whether a diffraction is Fresnel diffraction or Fraunhofer diffraction: Fresnel number(Equation (2)), some paper say if F≥1 we can approximately regard it as Fraunhofer diffraction, else if F<<1, we can only regard it as Fourier diffraction(some papers say the boundry is 0.203, but I don't think it's reliable, only for your reference).

#2.5. Air Gap and Rectangle Pinhole

In the actual industrial process, it is hard to make a circular pinhole in micrometers. So the pinhole we use is mostly rectangle one.

Air gap is a method we use to limit the receiving angle so that the size of the image is also be limited. Using the refractive index difference between air gap and its upper index, according to Fermat principle, the maximum receiving angle can be calculated. And the higher angles are totally reflected.

In this case, when the light go through a system with both rectangle pinhole and air gap, the shape of the image is not be decided by the shape of the pinhole but the existence of the air gap(or low refractive index layer), the image shape is circular(see Figure 6).

#3. Matrix Pinhole Imaging System(MAPIS)

#3.1. Why Matrix

Here I list the problem we may face if we use single pinhole:

  • Size of a single pinhole is limited, the energy it can collect is low.
  • The area we should detect is large and the image distance we can use is not enough.
  • Sometimes the area we need is not centrosymmetric.

Using matrix can perfectly solve these three problems, and in the meantime it may bring some new work on image preprocessing.

#3.2. How to Decide the Pinhole Pitch

When the geometrical point spread function is the main role in our imaging system(pinhole size is not too small), simply use the geometric relationship and Fermat principle may do it. If we get the data of refractive index of each layer in the whole imaging system, and the size of each pinhole image we would like to extract(in most cases, the energy in peripheral area is not enough to use), the pinhole pitch we should use can be calculated. And if we can add the divergence angle of the light source, it will be a plus to make it more precise. This pitch is not an accurate value, acceptable overlap or disjoint can determine its variation range. I have wrote a program to help us calculate it(See Figure 7 Pinhole Cycle is equal to Pinhole Pitch here). Also, by input some other data, the PPI(pixels per inch) and the Cell diameter on the image plane can be calculated.

So what kind of overlap is acceptable? In our calculation cases, when the boundary of the neighbor cell is not yet overlap the area we would like to extract, we think it is acceptable. But in some actual situation, the energy of the cell's boundary is very low so that its influence on the extracted area can be ignore. So this range can be a bit larger.

#4. Fingerprint on Display(FOD)

#4.1. Structure of FOD

FOD(Fingerprint on Display) is the principle project of VKS. The basic setup of FOD is shown on Figure 8 (in latest structure, TP is laid on Pol).Note that its data is not precise, only for reference.

Some tips in the structure of display(upper side from sensor):

  • TFT(Thin Film Transistor): TFT is the light source layer. The transmittance of common TFT is very low because it contains many circuit and metal. Our FOD project makes some little changes in TFT, the most efficiency way is that we engrave several pinholes in it.
  • TP(Touch Panel): TP contains high refractive index material(mostly ITO), which makes our image has big noise.
  • Pol(Polarizer): Pol is a circular polarizer which contains a line polarizer and a polarizer. Pol can make the outside light can't be reflected back to outside(See Figure 9).
  • The reflective index of each layer in this system except TP(about 2.0) and air gap(1.0) can ususally be regarded as 1.5.

#4.2. IRCUT

Considering ambient light, especially solar light, it is easier for long wavelength light diffraction through fingers go directly into our sensor. Red and infrared light act as crucial role in this phenomenon. So we deal with it by introducing IRCUT onto our sensor's cover glass (see Figure 10).

#4.3. TP on Pol & TP under Pol

For TP is a high reflection layer for us, The TP on Pol or under Pol seems make it big influence in the image we get from our sensor. This can be divded into two parts:

  • About object distance: in the case of TP under Pol, TP is closer to the TFT layer, which means the magnification of the light sources on TFT from TP to image plane is higher. So that we will see several glare points in our image.

  • About intensity: We know that Pol has a line polarizer which can reduce the intensity of the light(almost 50% for circularly polarized light). For the case of TP under Pol, the light from TFT to TP will not go through Pol, so that its intensity is much more higher than the case of TP on Pol.

Although TP on Pol seems good for our fingerprint imaging, it has disadvantage for display(about narrow border). So our latest display is TP under Pol. So we turn off several pixels around the pinhole to solve the glare point's problem.

#4.4. Ghosting image

In our simulation result, the output image should be like a matrix of circular cell. But in fact, each cell is not a perfect circle. It is more likely a overlay of several(two or more) circles(see Figure 11).

Right now no conclusion to what exactly causes this phenomenon. Here I list some of my guesses.

  • The reflection from TP or secondary reflection from the upper side of the IRCUT glass or upper side of the air gap.
  • The diffraction of pinhole.
  • Caused by pinhole's etching.

For further information of some of these guesses I have calculated please refer to my document.

#4.5. Anti-Dry Fingerprint Coating(ADFC)

Recognizing of dry finger is the most challenging problem for optical fingerprint recognition system. For dry finger especially in low temperature, the ridge of fingerprint can't fully touch the cover glass(CG). So that the imaging of dry fingerprint is Intermittent(see Figure 12). This stuff makes it hard for recognition algorithm to get the feature points resulting in a false recognition. For what we deal with is the CG of a mobile phone, we should consider many situations before we decided to make a change. Finally we decide to improve the contrast and the intensity of image by mainly adding a high refractive index coating on the top of CG(see Figure 13). And simply adding one high refractive index coating would improve the reflection of the interface of glass and coating, which is the noise for our imaging system. So by adding gradient or high/low（can be calculated using matrix） refractive index layers on the lower surface of high refractive coating(For the thickness of each layers is nanometers, the volatility of light should be considered), the image contrast could be much better. Although this method can't fundamentally solve this Dry Finger problem, things will be better by using ADFC and image processing algorithm related to it.
